### In the Beginning

A little over ten years ago, I was going through the online Code Academy curriculum. At the time, I was a small-town prosecutor in northeast Arizona.  After five years of living and lititigating there, it was time to move on. I had grown weary of sending the same people to prison again and again. Instead of looking for another legal job, I was considering jumping ship entirely and going in for a coding bootcamp. 

At the time, coding bootcamps were still growing, and so was the tech industry. Most of the bootcamps were either SF or New York City. I had lived in New York briefly, and I wanted to go back.

I worked through the online exercises at Code Academy. After I solved a particularly challenging one, I felt confident that I could make it work.  I applied to two bootcamps. One of them had a telephone interview as part of their admissions process. I'm not sure what they were looking for.  These were fly-by-night schools.  The first one I attended folder about two years after I graduated. 

I didn't have much of a plan after coding bootcamp. I did shockingly little research and even less planning on what I would do or go. I assumed that I would find a job somehow. With so little forethought, I floundered for a year or two to break into Tech. 

### Amazon

Today is my last day at Amazon. On January 28, 2026, they eliminated a couple thousand jobs of which mine was one. They didn't fire me, but they did eliminate my job. It's an important distinction that limits their liability. They gave me three months to find an internal transfer, which means no work and full pay for three months. I didn't look for a new job at Amazon, and I didn't find one. 

I welcomed and expected the layoff. For years there had been talk of "Return to Hub,"" which meant consolidating all the engineers into one of a few large offices. I was not living in one of the main cities, so I felt at risk. Even without Return to Hub, it felt time to go. I had been at Amazon for six years, and I felt, like I did as a prosecutor in rural Arizona, that it was time to move on.  

I thoroughly enjoyed my time at Amazon. I am immensely grateful for the opportunity that they extended me.  I don't have a computer science degree, adn yet they hired as a software engineer apprenticeship. When I started, I felt completely overwhelmed. I thought I was going to get fired at any moment. Somehow I didn't.

One of the reasons I lasted so long was my amazing managers. I was very fortunate to have managers who gave me a chance and supported me. I was also lucky to have great colleagues and coworkers. There were fun, smart, and supportive. 

I learned gobs as an engineer. I feel like working at Amazon was the equivalent of getting a computer science degree and an MBA. Working at Amazon for six years (3.5 as a softwware / 2.5 as security) was life changing. It has and will open up lots of doors.
