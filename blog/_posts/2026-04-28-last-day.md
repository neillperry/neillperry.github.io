### In the Beginning

A little over ten years ago, I was learning to code via the online Code Academy curriculum. At the time, I was a small-town prosecutor in northeast Arizona.  After five years of living and litigating there, it was time to move on. I had grown weary of sending the same people to prison again and again. Instead of looking for another legal job, I was considering jumping ship entirely and going in for a coding bootcamp. 

At the time, coding bootcamps were still fairly new, and the tech industry was on its uninterrupted rise. Most of the bootcamps at that time were either SF or New York City. I had lived in New York briefly, so I applied to programs there.

I had worked through the online exercises on the Code Academy website. After I solved a particularly challenging one, I felt confident that I could make it work. So I applied to two bootcamps. One of them had a telephone interview as part of their admissions process. I'm not sure what they were looking for, but I guess I passed.  These were fly-by-night schools. The first one I attended folded about two years after I graduated. 

I used up a big chunk of my life savings to attend these bootcamps. I didn't have much of a plan after the coding bootcamps just the vague notion that I would go write code for someone. I did shockingly little research and even less planning on what I would do or go after the bootcamp. I assumed that I would find a job somehow. With so little forethought, I floundered for a year or two to break into Tech. 

### Amazon

Today is my last day at Amazon. On January 28, 2026, the company eliminated 14,000 jobs of which mine was one. They didn't fire me, just deleted my job. They gave impacted employees a three-month grace period to find a new job at the company. For those that couldn't find an internal transfer, their employment would end.  

I had expected the layoff. For years there had been talk of "Return to Hub," which meant consolidating all the engineers into one of four U.S. cities. I was not living in one of those cities, so I knew that I was at risk of downsizing.   

I didn't look for a lateral transfer. Instead, I used the three-month grace period to move out and move on. I gave away my IKEA furniture and moved out of my apartment. It was time to leave. I had been at Amazon for six years, and I felt like I had done about everything that I had wanted to do. I worked first as a software engineer, then as a cybersecurity engineer.  

I thoroughly enjoyed my time at Amazon. I loved the people and my team. I am immensely grateful for the opportunity that Amazon extended me. I don't come from a traditional coding background nor do I have a computer science degree, and yet they hired me as a software engineer apprentice. When I started, I felt completely overwhelmed by how little I knew and how much I had to learn. I thought I was going to get fired at any moment. Somehow I didn't.

One of the reasons I lasted so long was my amazing managers. I was very fortunate to have managers who gave me a chance and supported me. I was also lucky to have great colleagues and coworkers. There were fun, smart, and supportive. 

I learned gobs as an engineer. I feel like working at Amazon was the equivalent of getting a computer science degree and an MBA. Working at Amazon for six years was life changing. It has and will open up lots of doors.

My three-month grace period and my Amazon tenure ended today. I am grateful to get hired and fired by Amazon. The timing of both those incidents felt right to me. So now it's on to something new.
