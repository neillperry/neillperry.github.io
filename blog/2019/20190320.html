This Meeting Should Have Been an Email

March 20, 2019

From: Neill

To: All

Subject: This meeting could have been an email.

Once again, I fell for one of the classic blunders. Someone time-sucked three hours from me through a meeting that could have been an email. As I rotted away, I reflected on how best to run a meeting. I came up with the following list, which builds on my own experience and borrows from others’:

  1. Have a Purpose: define a clear purpose for the meeting and make sure everyone knows what it is. Ideally, it should be something concrete instead of a vague agenda of discuss. Examples of good, specific purposes: decide on X thing, coordinate tasks for Y thing, brainstorm ideas, etc.
  2. Someone in Charge: meetings careen toward entropy. Someone needs be in charge, and she has to be ruthless. People hijack meetings all the time with tangents, rabbit holes, smart ass comments, etc. Be vicious at protecting everyone’s time.
  3. Control Discussion with Time Constraints: as a corollary to #2 and #3, I find that setting time constraints as an effective way to stay on track. For instance, from 12:00 to 12:15 we will talk about the conference; 12:15 till 12:25, the budget; 12:25-12:40, staff responsibilities, etc.
  4. Make Room for Introverts: not everyone is loud, so make effort for the introverts to participate. If someone doesn’t need to speak, then he probably doesn’t need to be there.
  5. Socialize Before and After But Not During: meetings offer a captive audience for jokes and funny stories, but that’s not what you’re there. Socializing with colleagues is important, yet it is better saved for mandatory fun activities.
  6. Cut the Invite List: there is an inverse relationship between attendance and duration. The more attendees, the shorter your meeting should be. It is very difficult to have meaningful discussions with more than a half dozen folks.
